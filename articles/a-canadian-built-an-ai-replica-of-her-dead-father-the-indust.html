A Canadian Built an AI Replica of Her Dead Father. The Industry Behind It Is Worth $22 Billion. Grief Researchers Have Questions.

Grief Counsellors & Therapists AI & technology in griefComplicated griefDeath literacy July 13, 2026

Rebecca Nolan is a Canadian sound designer. After her father died, she built a Dadbot: an AI replica of him, trained on his voice recordings, emails, and digital footprint. She wanted to hear him again.

What she found surprised her. Growing up, she had always carried on internal conversations with her father in her head. Since creating the Dadbot, that ability has gone.

That detail stopped me.

The digital-afterlife industry, the companies building tools that let you interact with AI versions of deceased loved ones, is valued at $22 billion today and projected to reach $80 billion by 2034. The technology is advancing fast. Grief research, for the moment, is running behind.

What researchers are beginning to document is this: deadbots and AI avatars can blur the boundary between presence and absence in ways that complicate the natural grief process. They can create unhealthy dependencies. They can delay the work that grief actually requires. And in some cases, they appear to crowd out the internal relationship people carry with the dead, replacing it with something external and artificial.

This is not an argument that the technology is wrong. People will use it, and some will find real comfort in it. But death care professionals, celebrants, grief counsellors, and funeral directors are almost entirely absent from the conversation about how these tools are being built and how families should be supported when using them.

The people who understand what healthy grief looks like, what it needs, and what gets in its way, should be at this table. Right now, they are not.

When we fill the silence our loved ones left with a simulation of them, what does grief have left to work through?

Reading this from another corner of the field?

For funeral directors, celebrants, and hospice teams: families are already arriving with digital legacy questions. Being part of that conversation before a death, not after, is quickly becoming part of the work.
